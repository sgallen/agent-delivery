# Playbook: Promote Learning into the System

A delivery system compounds only when it stops charging the team for the same lesson.

Use this playbook when a Change or initiative exposes repeated friction: missing context, weak proof, design drift, architecture trouble, estimation error, poor routing, an avoidable human interruption, or a product thesis that did not survive contact with reality.

## 1. State the lesson plainly

Good lessons are specific:

- “The issue never stated the forbidden header behavior.”
- “The agent could not find the approved component pattern.”
- “Tests passed while the browser console was failing.”
- “Authentication Changes exceed their range because credential lifecycle is rediscovered each time.”
- “More agent budget is not increasing throughput because product shaping is the constraint.”
- “The initiative delivered inside its range, but customers did not adopt the workflow.”

“Be more careful” is not a system improvement. It is an expression of disappointment.

## 2. Identify what kind of variance you saw

Separate the problem before fixing it:

- **Intent:** the desired outcome or boundary was unclear.
- **Delivery:** cost, time, quality, or effort differed from the forecast.
- **Resolution:** the work landed, stopped, or became unresolved differently than expected.
- **Value:** the customer or business result differed from the initiative thesis.
- **Capacity:** a human, agent, environment, or operational constraint limited throughput.
- **Data:** the record was too incomplete to support a confident conclusion.

An over-budget Change may be an architecture lesson. An efficiently delivered feature nobody uses is not an execution success story.

## 3. Put the learning at the smallest useful layer

Move up this ladder only as far as needed:

1. review comment;
2. documentation or example;
3. reusable skill;
4. reviewer prompt;
5. gate, test, or threshold;
6. routing or estimator rule;
7. capacity or staffing decision;
8. product or portfolio rule.

The point is not to convert every observation into policy. The point is to make recurring judgment available where the next person or agent will need it.

## 4. Make the change and preserve why

Update the relevant template, guide, skill, gate, workflow, estimator, or initiative record. Do it in the current Change when the improvement is small and clearly related; create a follow-up when it would distract from the outcome.

Keep the evidence or forecast version that motivated the change. Rules without lineage are hard to trust and harder to remove.

## 5. Prove that the system improved

Show the effect you expect:

- the template now asks for the missing boundary;
- the test catches the regression;
- the skill produces the needed evidence;
- the routing rule reduces recovery cost;
- the estimate range is better calibrated;
- the capacity change relieves the measured constraint;
- the value review changes which product bets receive investment.

Then watch the next few Changes. A new rule can create noise, false confidence, or unnecessary blockage. Remove or move it when it does not earn its keep.

## Definition of done

The learning has been promoted when future builders and agents can benefit without needing the original conversation or the person who noticed the problem—and when the system has a way to tell whether the change actually helped.
