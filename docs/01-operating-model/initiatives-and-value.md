# Initiatives and the Value Loop

A Change tells you what one piece of work took.

An initiative asks the harder question:

> Was the larger bet worth making?

An initiative is a meaningful product investment—a major feature, a platform capability, a reliability program, a new product line, or another coherent bet likely to require several Changes and enough commitment to deserve an explicit thesis.

Not every Change needs one. Routine maintenance should not require a miniature investment committee.

## Treat the initiative as a hypothesis

The loop is straightforward:

```text
opportunity
  → value hypothesis
  → investment range and stop conditions
  → discovery, delivery, experiments, and decisions
  → actual resource use and outcomes
  → release or evidence-backed conclusion
  → later value review
  → learning and reallocation
```

Delivery completion is a milestone. It is not the final answer.

A team can build the wrong thing cheaply and on time. It can also spend a modest amount proving that a promising idea should not continue. The operating model needs to recognize both.

## Start with the value you expect

Before committing meaningful resources, write down:

- the problem or opportunity;
- who should benefit;
- the customer, business, operational, or strategic value expected;
- the evidence supporting that belief;
- the baseline and the indicators that should move;
- when the value should appear;
- confidence and important unknowns;
- what evidence would cause the team to stop, narrow, or change course.

Value does not have to mean immediate revenue. It may be retention, customer time saved, lower risk, lower operating cost, reliability, market access, strategic learning, or a reusable platform capability.

Avoid two equal and opposite mistakes: forcing every initiative into a dubious revenue number, or writing “strategic value” and hoping nobody asks what that means.

## Forecast the complete investment

A useful forecast includes more than implementation:

```text
customer discovery and shaping
+ product, design, and architecture judgment
+ agent execution
+ builder and specialist review
+ execution infrastructure
+ rollout and operational support
+ continuing ownership
```

Use ranges, confidence, assumptions, and stop conditions. Preserve the opportunity, post-discovery, post-shaping, and in-flight forecasts rather than polishing the history afterward.

The forecast should help answer:

- What human capabilities will the work need?
- How much agent and infrastructure capacity can the team usefully absorb?
- What review and recovery load should we expect?
- Which constraint is most likely to matter?
- What would justify another tranche of investment?
- What would tell us to stop?

Do not assume every planned Change will land. Some may deliver capability. Some may answer an important question. Some may close for an external reason. Some may fail to resolve well. Forecasting that funnel honestly is more useful than treating a ticket list as a promise.

## Roll actual work into the initiative

Each linked Change should contribute its outcome, evidence, material resource use, important variance, and whether anything landed or released.

The initiative record should summarize rather than duplicate every run. The Change Records remain the evidence.

At useful checkpoints, compare:

```text
forecast investment    ↔ actual investment
expected Change path   ↔ actual outcomes
expected capability    ↔ delivered capability or decision evidence
expected constraint    ↔ the constraint that actually appeared
```

A material variance should produce an explanation and a decision, not merely a red number in a dashboard.

## Stopping can be the right outcome

An initiative may conclude without release because customer evidence weakened the thesis, a prototype exposed a bad workflow, technical investigation found an unacceptable constraint, the economics stopped making sense, or a better alternative appeared.

That can be a good investment in clarity.

It only earns that description when the evidence is preserved, the uncertainty is named, the decision is explicit, and the work changes what happens next.

A half-finished initiative that loses sponsorship is an administrative closure. One that simply fades out is unresolved loss. Do not make either sound more noble than it was.

Counterfactuals can still help. “This experiment probably avoided a much larger commitment” may be directionally important. Keep the assumptions and confidence attached; do not book hypothetical savings as realized value.

## Revisit value after delivery

Choose review points that fit the promised horizon—perhaps 30, 90, 180, and 365 days, or something specific to the product.

For released initiatives, ask:

- Did people adopt it?
- Did the customer outcome move from the baseline?
- Did business or operational value appear?
- What continuing human, agent, and infrastructure cost emerged?
- What unintended consequences showed up?
- How confident are we that the initiative caused the result?
- Should we expand, adapt, hold, stop, or retire it?

For initiatives concluded without release, ask whether later evidence supported the decision, whether the avoided commitment was actually avoided, whether the findings were reused, and whether the same uncertainty had to be rediscovered elsewhere.

Keep direct attribution, influenced outcomes, proxies, and counterfactual estimates separate. Product value is often delayed and noisy. Honest uncertainty is more useful than a clean fiction.

## Learn from two sides of the bet

The initiative creates two main feedback loops:

```text
Delivery variance
  What did the work take compared with what we expected?

Value variance
  What value appeared compared with the original thesis?
```

The resolution path matters inside both, especially when experiments or investigations shaped the final decision.

A team may estimate delivery well and choose weak opportunities. Another may identify valuable opportunities and repeatedly underestimate the cost of pursuing them. A third may deliver cheaply because it avoids the hard parts that would have created value.

Do not let “on time and on budget” end the conversation.

## Include the cost of owning the result

Launch is not the end of the investment.

Where material, include agent maintenance, cloud and data infrastructure, support load, operational ownership, compliance and security obligations, incident risk, migration, and retirement.

A feature that creates revenue while consuming nearly as much ongoing support is a different product bet from one with a light operational footprint.

## Rule

Treat an initiative as an investment hypothesis, not a folder full of tickets.

Preserve what the team expected, what the work took, what happened, what value appeared later, and what the next decision should learn from the difference.
